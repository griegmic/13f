How to read this tab This is the combined quarter-end portfolio of all 20 funds — every security anyone held, added up.
  • Total Value — combined dollar size of the position across all funds. Big number = where the most money sits.
  • # Funds — how many of the 20 funds hold it. This is the conviction signal: 15 funds holding Meta means broad agreement; one fund holding a $40B position means one whale (usually Berkshire).
  • Shares — combined share count, useful for comparing against the value.
Remember these are quarter-end snapshots published 45 days late — a fund may have traded since. One company can also appear under multiple CUSIPs (share classes — e.g. an ADR and the ordinary shares are separate rows).

How to read this tab The assets the 20 funds put the most new money into this quarter, ranked by estimated buying (Δ shares × quarter-end price). Price moves don't count — a stock that rallied isn't a "buy" unless funds actually added shares.
  • Est. Flow — estimated dollars of actual buying. This is the ranking.
  • % of All Buying — this asset's share of all the money funds added this quarter. 10% means one in every ten new dollars went here.
  • Δ Value — raw position change incl. price moves. If it's red while flow is green, funds were buying a falling stock — often the strongest conviction signal here.
  • NEW — a position opened from zero this quarter: the fund had none, now it does.
  • CUSIP Δ — likely not real buying: the issuer name also exited under another CUSIP (share-class switch), so the "buy" here usually pairs with a fake "sell" on the other side.
How to read this tab The assets the 20 funds pulled the most money out of this quarter, biggest sells first (Δ shares × quarter-end price). Price moves don't count — a stock that dropped isn't a "sell" unless funds actually unloaded shares.
  • Est. Flow — estimated dollars of actual selling. This is the ranking.
  • % of All Selling — this asset's share of all the money funds pulled out this quarter. 10% means one in every ten dollars sold came from here.
  • Δ Value — raw position change incl. price moves. If it's green while flow is red, funds were taking profits on a rising stock. If both are deep red, they were fleeing a falling one — the more bearish read.
  • EXITED — a position fully closed this quarter, the strongest sell signal in 13F data.
  • CUSIP Δ — likely not real selling: the issuer name also appears new under another CUSIP (share-class switch), so the "sell" here usually pairs with a fake "buy" on the other side.
Caveat: stock splits inflate share counts without any trading and can fake a large "sell" or "buy."
How to read this tab Securities that none of the 20 funds held last quarter and at least one holds now — fresh money into fresh names, ranked by the size of the new stake. Because this is the combined view, a stock one fund already owned that another fund just bought won't appear here (that shows up as buying on the Biggest Buys tab instead).
  • Est. Flow — the full size of the new position (a new stake is pure inflow).
  • % of All Buying — the new stake's share of all money funds added this quarter.
  • Most brand-new names are small starter positions — the interesting ones are large flows, which mean a fund opened with conviction rather than dipping a toe.
  • CUSIP Δ — the same issuer name also appears on the Exited tab under a different CUSIP. Usually a share-class switch or re-listing (e.g. AstraZeneca's ADR → ordinary shares), not a real new position — though for ETF trust umbrella names it can be two genuinely different funds.
How to read this tab Securities the funds held last quarter and hold zero of now — fully closed out across all 20 funds, ranked by money removed. A full exit is the strongest sell signal 13F data offers: someone didn't trim, they left. Partial sells live on the Biggest Sells tab.
  • Est. Flow — the prior position value, all of it withdrawn.
  • % of All Selling — the exit's share of all money funds pulled out this quarter.
  • Prev shows what the position was worth before the exit; Now is always $0.
  • CUSIP Δ — the same issuer name also appears on the New Positions tab under a different CUSIP. Usually a share-class switch or re-listing, not a real exit — though for ETF trust umbrella names it can be two genuinely different funds.
How to read this tab This compares two quarters and separates what funds did from what the market did. A position's value can change for two reasons — funds traded, or the price moved — and this tab splits them apart.
  • Est. Flow — the money funds actually added or removed by trading: share change × quarter-end price. Green = funds bought, red = funds sold. This is the "where is money going" number. (Flow percentages live on the Biggest Buys / Biggest Sells tabs, where they're measured against all buying or all selling.)
  • Δ Value — the raw change in position value. It mixes trading with price moves, so it can disagree with the flow. That disagreement is informative:
    Green flow + red Δ Value → funds bought a falling stock (buying the dip). Example: Apple Q1 2026 — funds added 8.5M shares (+$2.2B), but the price drop still pulled the position down $3.6B.
    Red flow + green Δ Value → funds trimmed a rising stock (taking profits).
  • Δ Shares — the raw share change behind the flow estimate.
  • NEW / EXITED — position opened or fully closed this quarter.
Caveats: stock splits change share counts without any trading and will fake a big flow — 13F data alone can't detect them. And when a company changes CUSIP (share-class switch, re-listing), it shows up as a fake sell + fake buy pair — those rows carry a CUSIP Δ badge.
The Biggest Buys and Biggest Sells tabs are this same table filtered to only inflows or only outflows, ranked by size.

Data: SEC EDGAR 13F-HR filings. Values are quarter-end positions, published 45 days after quarter close — a snapshot, not real-time. Long positions only; short positions are not disclosed in 13Fs.
Note on deltas: Est. Flow is the money actually added/removed by trading (Δ shares × quarter-end price); Δ Value is the raw change, which also includes price moves. Flow estimates can be distorted by stock splits (share counts change without any trading) and by mixed share/principal reporting.
